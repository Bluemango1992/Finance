Here is the TL;DR of the other "gold standard" finance models:

  - Comps (Comparables): Valuing a company by looking at what similar companies
    are worth (like pricing a house based on the house next door).
  - LBO (Leveraged Buyout): The model for buying a company using mostly borrowed
    money (like flipping a house using a mortgage).
  - MPT (Modern Portfolio Theory): The mathematical proof for "don't put all
    your eggs in one basket." It mixes different stocks/bonds to lower your risk
    without killing your returns.
  - Black-Scholes: A hardcore math formula used by Wall Street to price stock
    options (betting on future stock prices).
  - DuPont Analysis: An X-ray for profits. It breaks down a company's return on
    equity to see if they are actually a good business, or just masking bad
    performance with too much debt.

The Cheat Sheet:

  - DCF & Comps = How much is this company worth?
  - CAPM & WACC = What is our target profit / cost of money?
  - LBO = How much debt can we use to buy this company?
  - MPT = How do I build a safe investment portfolio?

Knowing that all these models are flawed, the smartest investors in the
world—like Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger—don't throw the math in the
trash. But they also don't trust complex, 50-tab spreadsheets.

Their philosophy is simple: "It is better to be roughly right than precisely
wrong."

If I were building an investment strategy knowing the flaws of Wall Street's
"gold standards," here is the arsenal of models and mental frameworks I would
actually use:

1. The "Reverse DCF" (Expectations Investing)

  - Fixes the flaw of: Traditional DCF (guessing the future).
  - How to use it: Instead of guessing a company’s future growth to figure out
    what the stock price should be, you do the exact opposite. You take the
    current stock price and run the math backward to figure out what growth the
    market is expecting.
  - Why it works: You don't have to be a psychic. If a stock is trading at $100,
    the Reverse DCF might tell you: "For this company to be worth $100, it must
    grow sales by 25% every year for a decade." Then you just apply common sense
    and ask: "Is that actually possible?" If the answer is no, you don't buy the
    stock.

2. Free Cash Flow (FCF) Yield

  - Fixes the flaw of: DuPont Analysis and Comps (trusting accounting tricks).
  - How to use it: You ignore "Net Income" or "Earnings" because accountants can
    legally manipulate those numbers. Instead, you look purely at the company's
    bank account: How much hard, cold cash did the business generate after
    paying for all its expenses and factory upgrades? Divide that cash by the
    price of the company.
  - Why it works: You can fake profits on a tax return, but you cannot fake cash
    in the bank. If a company has a high FCF Yield (meaning it spits out a ton
    of actual cash compared to its price), it is usually a highly resilient
    business.

3. Triangulation (The "Football Field" Chart)

  - Fixes the flaw of: Relying on any single model.
  - How to use it: You run a DCF, you run a Comps model, and you look at what
    similar companies were bought for in the past. You chart them all on a graph
    (Wall Street calls this a "Football Field" chart because the bars look like
    yard lines).
  - Why it works: You look for the overlap. If your DCF says the company is
    worth 40-50, and Comps says 45-55, your "truth" is probably somewhere in
    that overlapping 45-50 window. It prevents you from being blinded by the
    bias of one single equation.

4. The Pre-Mortem (Stress Testing)

  - Fixes the flaw of: LBOs and MPT (the "everything will go perfect" delusion).
  - How to use it: Before you invest a dollar, you assume it is 5 years in the
    future and the company has gone totally bankrupt. You ask: "What killed it?"
    Did interest rates spike? Did a new competitor invent better tech? Did a
    pandemic hit? Then, you plug those nightmare scenarios into your
    spreadsheet.
  - Why it works: Wall Street builds "Base Cases" (what they hope happens).
    Survivors build "Worst Cases." If a company can survive your nightmare
    spreadsheet, it’s probably a safe investment.

5. The Ultimate Meta-Model: Margin of Safety

  - Fixes the flaw of: Human unpredictability and Black Swans (Black-Scholes).
  - How to use it: This was invented by Benjamin Graham, the godfather of value
    investing. Once you use your models to figure out a company is worth $100...
    you refuse to pay more than $60 for it.
  - Why it works: That 40% discount is your "Margin of Safety." It acts as a
    shock absorber. If your DCF math was wrong, or the CEO makes a stupid
    decision, or the economy crashes, you bought it so cheap that you still
    won't lose your shirt.

The Takeaway

Amateurs use financial models as crystal balls to predict exactly what a stock
will do.

Professionals use financial models as bullshit detectors. They use Reverse DCFs,
Cash Flow tracking, and Margin of Safety to figure out when the market is lying,
when a CEO is too optimistic, and when a stock is drastically overpriced.
